Bulgaria became a member of the United Nations in 1955 and since 1966 has been a non-permanent member of the Security Council three times, most recently from 2002 to 2003.[171] It was also among the founding nations of the Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe (OSCE) in 1975. Euro-Atlantic integration has been a priority since the fall of communism, although the communist leadership also had aspirations of leaving the Warsaw Pact and joining the European Communities by 1987.[172][173] Bulgaria signed the European Union Treaty of Accession on 25 April 2005,[174] and became a full member of the European Union on 1 January 2007.[106] In addition, it has a tripartite economic and diplomatic collaboration with Romania and Greece,[175] good ties with China[176] and Vietnam[177] and a historical relationship with Russia.[178][179][180][181]

Bulgaria deployed significant numbers of both civilian and military advisors in Soviet-allied countries like Nicaragua[182] and Libya during the Cold War.[183] The first deployment of foreign troops on Bulgarian soil since World War II occurred in 2001, when the country hosted six KC-135 Stratotanker aircraft and 200 support personnel for the war effort in Afghanistan.[20] International military relations were further expanded with accession to NATO in March 2004[105] and the US-Bulgarian Defence Cooperation Agreement signed in April 2006. Bezmer and Graf Ignatievo air bases, the Novo Selo training range, and a logistics centre in Aytos subsequently became joint military training facilities cooperatively used by the United States and Bulgarian militaries.[184][185]

Domestic defence is the responsibility of the all-volunteer Bulgarian armed forces, composed of land forces, navy and an air force. The land forces consist of two mechanized brigades and eight independent regiments and battalions; the air force operates 106 aircraft and air defence systems in six air bases, and the navy operates various ships, helicopters and coastal defence weapons.[186] Active troops dwindled from 152,000 in 1988[187] to 31,300 in 2017, supplemented by 3,000 reservists and 16,000 paramilitary.[188] The inventory consists mostly of Soviet equipment like Mikoyan MiG-29 and Sukhoi Su-25 jets,[189] S-300PT air defence systems[190] and SS-21 Scarab short-range ballistic missiles.[191]

Bulgaria has an open, upper middle income range market economy where the private sector accounts for more than 70% of GDP.[192][193] From a largely agricultural country with a predominantly rural population in 1948, by the 1980s Bulgaria had transformed into an industrial economy with scientific and technological research at the top of its budgetary expenditure priorities.[194] The loss of COMECON markets in 1990 and the subsequent "shock therapy" of the planned system caused a steep decline in industrial and agricultural production, ultimately followed by an economic collapse in 1997.[195][196] The economy largely recovered during a period of rapid growth several years later,[195] but the average salary of 1,036 leva ($615) per month remains the lowest in the EU.[197] More than a fifth of the labour force are employed on a minimum wage of $1.16 per hour.[198]

A balanced budget was achieved in 2003 and the country began running a surplus the following year.[199] Expenditures amounted to $21.15 billion and revenues were $21.67 billion in 2017.[200] Most government spending on institutions is earmarked for security. The ministries of defence, the interior and justice are allocated the largest share of the annual government budget, whereas those responsible for the environment, tourism and energy receive the least amount of funding.[201] Taxes form the bulk of government revenue[201] at 30% of GDP.[202] Bulgaria has some of the lowest corporate income tax rates in the EU at a flat 10% rate.[203] The tax system is two-tier. Value added tax, excise duties, corporate and personal income tax are national, whereas real estate, inheritance, and vehicle taxes are levied by local authorities.[204] Strong economic performance in the early 2000s reduced government debt from 79.6% in 1998 to 14.1% in 2008.[199] It has since increased to 28.7% of GDP by 2016, but remains the third lowest in the EU.[205]

The Yugozapaden planning area is the most developed region with a per capita gross domestic product (PPP) of $26,580 in 2016.[206] It includes the capital city and the surrounding Sofia Province, which alone generate 42% of national gross domestic product despite hosting only 22% of the population.[207][3] PPP GDP per capita and the cost of living in 2017 stood at 49 and 48.4% of the EU average, respectively.[208][209] National PPP GDP was estimated at $143.1 billion in 2016, with a per capita value of $20,116.[210] Economic growth statistics take into account illegal transactions from the informal economy, which is the largest in the EU as a percentage of economic output.[211][212] The Bulgarian National Bank issues the national currency, lev, which is pegged to the euro at a rate of 1.95583 levа per euro.[213]

After several consecutive years of high growth, repercussions of the financial crisis of 2007–2008 resulted in a 3.6% contraction of GDP in 2009 and increased unemployment.[214][215] Industrial output declined 10%, mining by 31%, and ferrous and metal production marked a 60% drop.[216] Positive growth was restored in 2010 but intercompany debt exceeded $59 billion, meaning that 60% of all Bulgarian companies were mutually indebted.[217] By 2012, it had increased to $97 billion, or 227% of GDP.[218] The government implemented strict austerity measures with IMF and EU encouragement to some positive fiscal results, but the social consequences of these measures, such as increased income inequality and accelerated outward migration, have been "catastrophic" according to the International Trade Union Confederation.[219]

Siphoning of public funds to the families and relatives of politicians from incumbent parties has resulted in fiscal and welfare losses to society.[220][221] Bulgaria ranks 71st in the Corruption Perceptions Index[222] and experiences the worst levels of corruption in the European Union, a phenomenon that remains a source of profound public discontent.[223][224] Along with organized crime, corruption has resulted in a rejection of the country's Schengen Area application and withdrawal of foreign investment.[225][226][227] Government officials reportedly engage in embezzlement, influence trading, government procurement violations and bribery with impunity.[158] Government procurement in particular is a critical area in corruption risk. An estimated 10 billion leva ($5.99 billion) of state budget and European cohesion funds are spent on public tenders each year;[228] nearly 14 billion ($8.38 billion) were spent on public contracts in 2017 alone.[229] A large share of these contracts are awarded to a few politically connected[230] companies amid widespread irregularities, procedure violations and tailor-made award criteria.[231] Despite repeated criticism from the European Commission,[227] EU institutions refrain from taking measures against Bulgaria because it supports Brussels on a number of issues, unlike Poland or Hungary.[223]

The labour force is 3.36 million people,[232] of whom 6.8% are employed in agriculture, 26.6% in industry and 66.6% in the services sector.[233] Extraction of metals and minerals, production of chemicals, machine building, steel, biotechnology, tobacco and food processing and petroleum refining are among the major industrial activities.[234][235][236] Mining alone employs 24,000 people and generates about 5% of the country's GDP; the number of employed in all mining-related industries is 120,000.[237][238] Bulgaria is Europe's fifth-largest coal producer.[238][239] Local deposits of coal, iron, copper and lead are vital for the manufacturing and energy sectors.[240]

Two-thirds of food and agricultural exports go to OECD countries.[241] Although cereal and vegetable output dropped by 40% between 1990 and 2008,[242] output has since increased, and the 2016–2017 season registered the biggest grain output in a decade.[243][244] Maize, barley, oats and rice are also grown. Quality Oriental tobacco is a significant industrial crop.[245] Bulgaria is also the largest producer globally of lavender and rose oil, both widely used in fragrances.[20][246][247][248] Of the services sector, tourism is a significant contributor to economic growth. Bulgaria has emerged as a travelling destination with its inexpensive resorts and beaches outside the reach of the tourist industry.[249] Most of the visitors are Romanian, German, Turkish, British and Russian.[250] Sofia, Plovdiv, Veliko Tarnovo, coastal resorts Golden Sands and Sunny Beach and winter resorts Bansko, Pamporovo and Borovets are some of the locations most visited by tourists.[251]

Spending on research and development amounts to 0.78% of GDP,[252] and the bulk of public R&D funding goes to the Bulgarian Academy of Sciences (BAS).[253] Private businesses accounted for more than 73% of R&D expenditures and employed 42% of Bulgaria's 22,000 researchers in 2015.[254] The same year, Bulgaria ranked 39th out of 50 countries in the Bloomberg Innovation Index, the highest score being in education (24th) and the lowest in value-added manufacturing (48th).[255] Chronic government underinvestment in research since 1990 has forced many professionals in science and engineering to leave Bulgaria.[256]

Despite the lack of funding, research in chemistry, materials science and physics remains strong.[253] Three per cent of economic output is generated by the information and communication technologies (ICT) sector where 40,000[257] to 51,000 software engineers are employed.[258] More than a quarter of them are women, the highest percentage of females in ICT in any EU country.[259] High levels of female participation are a legacy of the Soviet era,[260] when the country was known as a "Communist Silicon Valley" due to its key role in COMECON computing technology production.[261] Bulgaria is also a regional leader in high performance computing and operates Avitohol, the most powerful supercomputer in Southeast Europe.[262]

Bulgaria has made numerous contributions to space exploration.[263] These include two scientific satellites, more than 200 payloads and 300 experiments in Earth orbit, as well as two cosmonauts since 1971.[263] Bulgaria was the first country to grow wheat and vegetables in space with its Svet greenhouses on the Mir space station.[264][265] It was involved in the development of the Granat gamma-ray observatory[266] and the Vega program, particularly in modelling trajectories and guidance algorithms for both Vega probes.[267][268] Bulgarian instruments have been used in the exploration of Mars, including a spectrometer that took the first high quality spectroscopic images of Martian moon Phobos with the Phobos 2 probe.[263][266] Cosmic radiation en route to and around the planet has been mapped by Liulin-ML dosimeters on the ExoMars TGO.[269] Variants of these instruments have also been fitted to the Chandrayaan-1 lunar probe and the International Space Station.[270][271] Bulgaria's first geostationary communications satellite—BulgariaSat-1—was launched by SpaceX in June 2017.[272]

Telephone services are widely available, and a central digital trunk line connects most regions.[273] Vivacom (BTC) serves more than 90% of fixed lines and is one of the three operators providing mobile services, along with A1 and Telenor.[274][275] Internet penetration stood at 61.9% of the population aged 16–74, in 2017.[276]

Bulgaria's strategic geographic location and well-developed energy sector make it a key European energy centre despite its lack of significant fossil fuel deposits.[277] Thermal power plants generate 48.9% of electricity, followed by nuclear power from the Kozloduy reactors (34.8%) and renewable sources (16.3%).[278] Equipment for a second nuclear power station at Belene has been acquired, but the fate of the project remains uncertain.[279] Installed capacity amounts to 12,668 MW, allowing Bulgaria to exceed domestic demand and export energy.[280]

The national road network has a total length of 19,512 kilometres (12,124 mi),[281] of which 19,235 kilometres (11,952 mi) are paved. Railroads are a major mode of freight transportation, although highways carry a progressively larger share of freight. Bulgaria has 6,238 kilometres (3,876 mi) of railway track[273] and currently a total of 81 kilometres (50 miles) of high-speed lines are in operation.[282][283] Rail links are available with Romania, Turkey, Greece, and Serbia, and express trains serve direct routes to Kiev, Minsk, Moscow and Saint Petersburg.[284] Sofia and Plovdiv are the country's air travel hubs, while Varna and Burgas are the principal maritime trade ports.[273]

The population of Bulgaria is 7,360,000 people according to the 2011 national census. The majority of the population, or 72.5%, reside in urban areas.[285] As of 2017[update], Sofia is the most populated urban centre with 1,330,000 people, followed by Plovdiv (345,000), Varna (344,000), Burgas (209,000) and Ruse (160,000).[3] Bulgarians are the main ethnic group and comprise 84.8% of the population. Turkish and Roma minorities comprise 8.8 and 4.9%, respectively; some 40 smaller minorities comprise 0.7%, and 0.8% do not self-identify with an ethnic group.[286][287] Former Statistics head Reneta Indzhova has disputed the 2011 census figures, suggesting the actual population is smaller than reported and a higher percentage of citizens are of Romani origin.[288][289] The Roma minority is usually underestimated in census data and may represent up to 11% of the population.[290] Population density is 65 per square kilometre, almost half the European Union average.[291]

Bulgaria is in a state of demographic crisis.[292][293] It has had negative population growth since the early 1990s, when the economic collapse caused a long-lasting emigration wave.[294] Some 937,000 to 1,200,000 people—mostly young adults—left the country by 2005.[294][295] The total fertility rate (TFR) was estimated at 1.46 children born per woman in 2017.[296] The majority of children are born to unmarried women.[297] Furthermore, a third of all households consist of only one person and 75.5% of families do not have children under the age of 16.[293] The resulting birth rates are among the lowest in the world[298][299] while death rates are among the highest.[300]

High death rates result from a combination of an ageing population, a high number of people at risk of poverty and a weak healthcare system.[301] More than 80% of all deaths are due to cancer and cardiovascular conditions; nearly a fifth of those are avoidable.[302] Mortality rates can be sharply reduced to levels below the EU average through timely and adequate access to medical services, which the healthcare system does not provide fully.[303] Although healthcare in Bulgaria is nominally universal,[304] out-of-pocket expenses account for nearly half of all healthcare spending, which significantly limits access to medical care.[305] Other problems disrupting care provision are the emigration of doctors due to low wages, understaffed and under-equipped regional hospitals, supply shortages and frequent changes to the basic service package for those insured.[306][307] The 2018 Bloomberg Health Care Efficiency Index ranked Bulgaria last out of 56 countries.[308] Average life expectancy is 74.7 years compared with an EU average of 80.2 and a world average of 69.[309][310]

Bulgarian is the only language with official status and native for 85% of the population.[318] It belongs to the Slavic group of languages, but it has a number of grammatical peculiarities, shared with its closest relative Macedonian, that set it apart from other Slavic languages: these include a complex verbal morphology (which also codes for distinctions in evidentiality), the absence of noun cases and infinitives, and the use of a suffixed definite article.[319] Other major languages are Turkish and Romani, which according to the 2011 census were spoken natively by 9.1% and 4.2% respectively.

More than three-quarters of Bulgarians subscribe to Eastern Orthodoxy.[320] Sunni Muslims are the second-largest religious community and constitute 10% of Bulgaria's overall religious makeup, although a majority of them are not observant and find the use of Islamic veils in schools unacceptable.[321] Less than 3% of the population are affiliated with other religions and 11.8% are irreligious or do not self-identify with a religion.[320] The Bulgarian Orthodox Church gained autocephalous status in AD 927,[322][323] and has 12 dioceses and over 2,000 priests.[324] Bulgaria is a secular state with guaranteed religious freedom by constitution, but Orthodoxy is designated as a "traditional" religion.[325]

Contemporary Bulgarian culture blends the formal culture that helped forge a national consciousness towards the end of Ottoman rule with millennia-old folk traditions.[326] An essential element of Bulgarian folklore is fire, used to banish evil spirits and illnesses. Many of these are personified as witches, whereas other creatures like zmey and samodiva (veela) are either benevolent guardians or ambivalent tricksters.[327] Some rituals against evil spirits have survived and are still practised, most notably kukeri and survakari.[328] Martenitsa is also widely celebrated.[329] Nestinarstvo, a ritual fire-dance of Thracian origin, is included in the list of UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage.[330][331]

Nine historical and natural objects are UNESCO World Heritage Sites: Pirin National Park, Sreburna Nature Reserve, the Madara Rider, the Thracian tombs in Sveshtari and Kazanlak, the Rila Monastery, the Boyana Church, the Rock-hewn Churches of Ivanovo and the ancient city of Nesebar.[332] The Rila Monastery was established by Saint John of Rila, Bulgaria's patron saint, whose life has been the subject of numerous literary accounts since Medieval times.[333]

During the Middle Ages, the establishment of the Preslav and Ohrid literary schools in the 10th century is associated with a golden period in Bulgarian literature.[333] The schools' emphasis on Christian scriptures made the Bulgarian Empire a centre of Slavic culture, bringing Slavs under the influence of Christianity and providing them with a written language.[334][335][336] Its alphabet, Cyrillic script, was developed by the Preslav Literary School.[337] The Tarnovo Literary School, on the other hand, is associated with a Silver age of literature defined by high-quality manuscripts on historical or mystical themes under the Asen and Shishman dynasties.[333] Many literary and artistic masterpieces were destroyed by the Ottoman conquerors, and artistic activities did not re-emerge until the National Revival in the 19th century.[326] The enormous body of work of Ivan Vazov (1850–1921) covered every genre and touched upon every facet of Bulgarian society, bridging pre-Liberation works with literature of the newly established state.[333] Notable later works are Bay Ganyo by Aleko Konstantinov, the Nietzschean poetry of Pencho Slaveykov, the Symbolist poetry of Peyo Yavorov and Dimcho Debelyanov, the Marxist-inspired works of Geo Milev and Nikola Vaptsarov, and the Socialist realism novels of Dimitar Dimov and Dimitar Talev.[333] Tzvetan Todorov is a notable contemporary author,[338] while Bulgarian-born Elias Canetti was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1981.[339]

А religious visual arts heritage includes frescoes, murals and icons, many produced by the medieval Tarnovo Artistic School.[340] Like literature, it was not until the National Revival when Bulgarian visual arts began to reemerge. Zahari Zograf was a pioneer of the visual arts in the pre-Liberation era.[326] After the Liberation, Ivan Mrkvička, Anton Mitov, Vladimir Dimitrov, Tsanko Lavrenov and Zlatyu Boyadzhiev introduced newer styles and substance, depicting scenery from Bulgarian villages, old towns and historical subjects. Christo is the most famous Bulgarian artist of the 21st century, known for his outdoor installations.[326]

Folk music is by far the most extensive traditional art and has slowly developed throughout the ages as a fusion of Far Eastern, Oriental, medieval Eastern Orthodox and standard Western European tonalities and modes.[341] Bulgarian folk music has a distinctive sound and uses a wide range of traditional instruments, such as gadulka, gaida, kaval and tupan. A distinguishing feature is extended rhythmical time, which has no equivalent in the rest of European music.[20] The State Television Female Vocal Choir won a Grammy Award in 1990 for its performances of Bulgarian folk music.[342] Written musical composition can be traced back to the works of Yoan Kukuzel (c. 1280–1360),[343] but modern classical music began with Emanuil Manolov, who composed the first Bulgarian opera in 1890.[326] Pancho Vladigerov and Petko Staynov further enriched symphony, ballet and opera, with singers Ghena Dimitrova, Boris Christoff, Ljuba Welitsch and Nicolai Ghiaurov elevated to a world-class level.[326][344][345][346][347][348][349] Bulgarian performers have gained acclaim in other genres like electropop (Mira Aroyo), jazz (Milcho Leviev) and blends of jazz and folk (Ivo Papazov).[326]

The Bulgarian National Radio, bTV and daily newspapers Trud, Dnevnik and 24 Chasa are some of the largest national media outlets.[350] Bulgarian media were described as generally unbiased in their reporting in the early 2000s and print media had no legal restrictions.[351] Since then, freedom of the press has deteriorated to the point where Bulgaria scores 111th globally in the World Press Freedom Index, lower than all European Union members and membership candidate states. The government has diverted EU funds to sympathetic media outlets and bribed others to be less critical on problematic topics, while attacks against individual journalists have increased.[352][353] Collusion between politicians, oligarchs and the media is widespread.[352]

Bulgarian cuisine is similar to that of other Balkan countries and demonstrates strong Turkish and Greek influences.[354] Yogurt, lukanka, banitsa, shopska salad, lyutenitsa and kozunak are among the best-known local foods. Meat consumption is lower than the European average, given a cultural preference for a large variety of salads.[354] Bulgaria was the world's second-largest wine exporter until 1989, but has since lost that position.[355][356] The 2016 harvest yielded 128 million litres of wine, of which 62 million was exported mainly to Romania, Poland and Russia.[357] Mavrud, Rubin, Shiroka melnishka, Dimiat and Cherven Misket are the typical grapes used in Bulgarian wine.[358] Rakia is a traditional fruit brandy that was consumed in Bulgaria as early as the 14th century.[359]
